In his dialogue Hippias Major, Plato has Socrates debate the essence of beauty, exploring whether it is something simple or complex. While the discussion is ultimately inconclusive, it establishes that beauty possesses a reality similar to the canonical Forms, which are discovered through dialectical inquiry. Plato's dialogues also propose that beauty is closely related to the good, but not identical to it. This suggests that while beauty might appear simple, its true nature is a complex philosophical concept.

In the Tao Te Ching, Lao Tzu presents the profound beauty of simplicity as a return to one's source or true nature. The text contains verses that extol simplicity, patience, and compassion as the "three greatest treasures." Lao Tzu's vision is not one of naive simplicity, but rather a "simplicity on the other side of complexity," suggesting that true mastery lies in navigating and understanding complexity to arrive at a deeper, more elegant simplicity. For example, he advises, "See simplicity in the complicated, seek greatness in small things".

In his account of living simply in a cabin in the woods, Thoreau critiques the excessive detail and complexity of modern life. His famous advice, "Simplify, simplify," is a central theme of the work. He argues that by stripping away societal clutter, one can find a deeper beauty and understanding of existence.

In contrast to writing on simplicity, Heller's satirical novel Catch-22 showcases the absurd beauty of pure, unfettered complexity. The infamous "Catch-22" is a maddeningly convoluted bureaucratic rule with a logical-yet-insane internal consistency that perfectly encapsulates the chaotic complexity of wartime absurdity.
